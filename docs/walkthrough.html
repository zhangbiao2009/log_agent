log‑agent  /  a self-driving incident pipeline  /  Go 1.25

A firehose of logs in.
About a dozen diagnosed incidents out.

Engineers used to dig through logs from every service by hand, guess which one was the root cause, and guess at the fix. log-agent watches every service continuously, spots what's abnormal, groups a cascade into a single incident, and asks an LLM what broke and how to fix it — before anyone opens a dashboard.

What lands in the on-call channel
“bank-gateway stopped responding 2 minutes after its v2.3.1 deploy, cascading into connection failures in payment-service and timeouts in order-service. P1 — recommend rolling bank-gateway back to v2.3.0.”
6 layers, L1→L6 ~5–20 LLM calls / day $0 on the hot path in-memory, no database one binary

The shape

One pipeline per service, fanning in to one shared brain.

The cheap, high-volume work runs independently and in parallel for every service (L1–L3) — each with its own pattern tree and baselines, so a slow or failing source for one service never blocks the others. Their low-volume alert channels fan in to a single shared pipeline (L4–L6) that has to see everything at once to correlate a cross-service cascade.

How it works

Every layer is a filter. The stream narrows as it falls.

The whole design turns on one idea: do the expensive work as rarely as possible. Each layer cuts the volume before the next one sees it, so by the time a stream reaches the costly LLM call it has shrunk by several orders of magnitude. Read this top to bottom — the bands narrow because the data does.

L1

Ingest & Filter

Poll Loki or replay a file — one source per service. Drop every line that isn't an error before anything else touches it.

~90%dropped at the door
L2

Pattern fingerprint

Drain algorithm groups identical lines into templates, so you count kinds of errors, not lines. No regex to maintain — it learns templates from the stream.

thousands→fewlines to templates
L3

Anomaly detection

Compare each pattern to its own rolling baseline. Forward only three things: a spike (> mean + 3σ), a brand-new pattern, or a service-wide rate jump.

raremost windows stay silent
L4

Cross-service correlator

Group alerts that co-occur across services using the dependency graph. A cascade through three services becomes one incident, with the deepest service flagged as the likely root cause.

3→1cascade to incident
L5

LLM diagnosis

Hand the incident — log samples, dependency chain, recent deploys — to DeepSeek. Get back a root cause, a severity, and a concrete fix. This is the target the whole funnel protects.

5–20 / daynot per minute
L6

Lifecycle & notify

Track each incident OPEN → ONGOING → RESOLVED, suppress duplicate pings, auto-resolve when it goes quiet, and route by severity to Slack, Teams, or email.

right peopleno alert fatigue
⏲
Two stages are dams, not streams: the aggregator holds a ~1 min window, the correlator a ~2 min window. Back to back, that's why the LLM typically sees a fresh incident within ~3 minutes — the core latency-vs-signal trade-off. Everything else flows through instantly.

Follow one failure

A bad deploy, traced end to end.

The clearest way to understand the pipeline is to watch a single real cascade fall through it — the exact scenario the demo config replays.

14:30
The trigger

bank-gateway ships v2.3.1

A routine deploy goes out. Two minutes later, the gateway stops responding — and stops emitting logs entirely.

L1–3
Downstream services light up

payment-service can't reach it

Connection errors pour in. L2 collapses 200 near-identical lines into a single template; L3 sees the spike against a baseline of zero.

connection refused to bank-gw-prod-1:443, request_id=abc123 ×200/min
L1–3
The cascade spreads upstream

order-service starts timing out

Because it depends on payment-service, its own error rate jumps from a baseline of 2 to 50/min.

timeout calling payment-service: context deadline exceeded after 5s ×50/min
L4
Correlation

Three alerts → one incident

All three fire inside the correlation window and are linked in the dependency graph: order → payment → bank-gateway. The deepest service — bank-gateway — is flagged as the suspected root cause.

L5–6
Diagnosis & dispatch

The LLM writes the incident — and it gets routed

The incident, its samples, the dependency chain and the recent deploy go to the model. Its verdict comes back, gets deduped, and lands in the channels mapped to its severity:

See it yourself

The demo runs the whole cascade with no external deps.

The fastest way to make all of this concrete: build the binary and point it at the demo config, which replays the exact scenario above from local NDJSON files.

$ ./log-agent config/config-demo.yaml
